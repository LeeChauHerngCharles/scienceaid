Structures and Properties of Covalent Substances

Simple Covalent Structures

In simple covalent structures, the atoms are held together by strong covalent bonds. However, the molecules are held together by weak van deer Waals' forces. Van der Waals' forces are due to the temporary shift of electrons to a particular side of a molecule which causes slight positive and negative poles.

Volatility

Simple covalent sructures have low boiling and melting points as the weak van der Waals' forces are easily broken.

Solubility

Most simple covalent structures are soluble in organic solvents but not in water. Examples of organic solvents are oil, petrol and turpentine. Organic solvents have at least one carbon atom. There are a few exceptions to this, such as alcohol and sugar, which are soluble in water.

Electrical Conductivity

Most covalent elements do not conduct electricity in any state as they lack delocalised electrons or free mobile ions.

Giant Molecular Structures

Allotropes are different forms of the same element. Diamond and graphite are allotropes are carbon. Silicon(IV) oxide is a giant molecular structure.

Diamond

The carbon atoms in diamond are arranged in a tetrahedral shape. This accounts for its propertities as a hard substance. As the carbon atoms are held together by covalent bonds, it has a very high boiling and melting point.

Graphite

Carbon atoms in graphite are bonded in layers with 3 carbon atoms bonded to 1 carbon atom. The covalent bonds result in a large amount of energy required melt or boil it. The electron that remains unused enables graphite to conduct electricity. The layers of carbon in graphite are held together by weak van der Waals' forces. This accounts for it being soft and slippery.

Silicon (IV) oxide

Silicon(IV) oxide is a giant covalent structure. The ratio of oxygen to silicon atoms is 3:1, the atoms are arranged in a tetrahedral shape. This accounts for its propertities as a hard substance. As the atoms are held together by covalent bonds, it has a very high boiling and melting point.

Volatility

Most giant molecular structures have high melting and boiling points as the covalent bonds that hold them together require a large amount of energy to break.

Solubility

Giant molecular structures are insoluble in all solvents

Electrical Conductivity

Giant molecular structures are non conductors of electricity except for graphite. This is as graphite has a electron that is not used for bonding.
